**Q: I want to optimize the beauty of my offspring. How do I choose a mate to optimize for standard north american beauty?**

**A:** You cannot reliably engineer “beautiful offspring” by mate choice. The best practical strategy is to choose a partner you find attractive, healthy, developmentally normal, and highly compatible as a co-parent. Beauty is partly heritable, but it is polygenic, culturally variable, and heavily shaped by development, environment, grooming, nutrition, orthodontics, sleep, fitness, and social presentation.

For mainstream North American attractiveness, the relatively stable traits are:

| Target                      | Practical mate-selection proxy                                        | Caveat                                                       |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Facial proportionality      | Partner has facial balance, symmetry, harmonious features             | Child outcome is probabilistic, not deterministic            |
| Healthy appearance          | Clear skin, healthy hair/teeth, normal body composition, good posture | Many factors are lifestyle/developmental                     |
| Height/build                | Partner’s height/build and family pattern                             | Regression toward population average is common               |
| Attractive expression       | Warm facial expressiveness, social confidence                         | Strongly environmental/learned                               |
| Good developmental genetics | Low burden of severe heritable disorders; good family health history  | Use genetic counseling for serious family risks              |
| Cultural fit                | Traits you and your social environment perceive as attractive         | “North American standard” is not one fixed biological target |

Facial morphology has substantial genetic influence, but attractiveness judgments are not reducible to a simple gene list. Research on facial beauty repeatedly identifies symmetry, averageness, and sexual dimorphism as common contributors, but later work also shows disagreement and context dependence across raters and populations. ([PubMed][1]) Individual face preferences are also shaped heavily by personal experience rather than shared genetics alone. ([PMC][2])

Do not use race, ethnicity, or ancestry as a proxy. It is crude, socially loaded, and scientifically weak for this goal. Pick for actual observable traits, health, compatibility, and family patterns.

The highest-leverage “optimization” is not mate choice alone. It is:

1. Choose someone you are strongly attracted to.
2. Confirm long-term compatibility, emotional stability, and parenting quality.
3. Avoid close genetic relatedness.
4. Screen for serious heritable disease if family history warrants it.
5. Maximize the child’s developmental environment: nutrition, sleep, dental care, exercise, sunlight balance, skin care, confidence, socialization, and grooming.

Avoid cosmetic genetic selection schemes. The American Society for Reproductive Medicine states that polygenic embryo testing should not be used for nonmedical trait selection, and current polygenic screening has major scientific and ethical limits. ([ASRM][3])

Bottom line: choose a mate you personally find beautiful, healthy, kind, intelligent, emotionally stable, and compatible. That gives the best combined probability of attractive, healthy, well-adjusted children.

**Q: Using the golden triangle as a measure, if my facial proportions fall just outside this area will choosing a mate with a smashed face average closer to the target?**

**A:** No. In a crude **one-dimensional** model, an opposite-trait mate can shift the expected child average toward the midpoint. But for whole-face attractiveness, choosing someone with a strongly “smashed” or recessed face is not a reliable correction strategy.

A better model:

`child trait ≈ mid-parent average + random genetic recombination + developmental/environmental noise`

So if your face is slightly outside a target zone, a mate with a **mild, specific, complementary trait** could shift the expected average. But an extreme counter-trait can also produce disharmony: recessed midface, weak jaw projection, bite issues, airway issues, dental crowding, or a combination of mismatched proportions.

The “golden triangle” / golden-ratio approach should also be treated as a loose aesthetic heuristic, not a real optimization function. A 2024 systematic review found that attractive faces show general harmony, but also that codified golden-ratio facial rules are unclear and that the golden ratio was not significantly associated with facial evaluation scores across ethnicities. ([Augusta University Research Profiles][1])

For mate choice, the more rational filter is:

| Goal                                | Better selection criterion                                                                  |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Move closer to balanced proportions | Partner has balanced, non-extreme proportions                                               |
| Improve facial harmony              | Partner’s features are attractive as a whole, not merely opposite yours                     |
| Reduce risk of bad trait stacking   | Avoid extreme retrusion/protrusion, severe asymmetry, major malocclusion                    |
| Improve offspring odds              | Choose health, normal development, good teeth/jaw/airway, good skin/hair, and compatibility |

Facial structure is highly polygenic. A 2025 genome-wide association study found hundreds of genetic loci associated with facial variation, but their combined predictive power remains limited; the study’s identified single nucleotide polymorphisms explained only up to 7.9% of facial variation per trait. ([Nature][2])

Practical answer: do **not** select an extreme opposite face to “average out” your own proportions. Select a mate whose face is already attractive, harmonious, and healthy by your target standard, with mild complementarity where relevant.

**Q: I've seen an attractive couple breed dog-like offspring and vice versa. You're saying a beautiful partner is enough? Should the male be a more attractive male to females than vice versa?**

**A:** No. A beautiful partner is **not enough**. It only improves the probability distribution.

Attractive parents can produce less-attractive children because facial outcome is not a clean average. It is affected by recombination, dominance, sex-specific expression, developmental variation, orthodontic growth, nutrition, hormones, and feature compatibility. Face shape is highly polygenic; a 2025 genome-wide association study identified 253 independent single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) across 188 loci, but even those explained only up to 7.9% of variation per facial trait. ([Nature][1])

The better rule is:

**Choose a mate who is attractive, harmonious, healthy, non-extreme, and comes from a family where attractive traits appear consistently across siblings/parents.**

On the male-vs-female question: **no, there is no reliable rule that the male should be “more attractive to females” than the female is to males.** Both parents matter. Facial traits show sex-specific genetic effects, meaning the same inherited variation may express differently in sons and daughters. ([PMC][2]) Also, sexual dimorphism in faces varies substantially across populations, so “more masculine male” or “more feminine female” is not a universal optimization target. ([PMC][3])

A practical mate-selection hierarchy would be:

| Priority | What to look for                                                                           |
| -------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1        | Broadly attractive face, not just one standout feature                                     |
| 2        | Facial harmony: jaw, midface, nose, eyes, forehead, lips, cheekbones work together         |
| 3        | Low asymmetry, good dental/jaw development, no severe retrusion/protrusion                 |
| 4        | Attractive relatives, especially siblings, because that shows trait recombination patterns |
| 5        | Good health, skin, hair, posture, body composition                                         |
| 6        | Co-parenting compatibility and intelligence                                                |

If you are male and want attractive children, the best female mate is not necessarily an extreme beauty archetype. It is usually a woman with **balanced feminine facial structure**, healthy development, good teeth/jaw/airway, attractive siblings/parents, and no extreme counter-traits that could combine badly with yours.

If you are female choosing a male, same logic: choose a man with attractive masculine structure, but avoid overly extreme features if they may create disharmony in daughters.

Bottom line: optimize for **harmonious average-plus traits**, not “maximum beauty” or “opposite-trait correction.” Extreme features increase variance. Balanced attractive families are the safer signal.

**Q: What if her entire family is beautiful but there is a dog-face found?**

**A:** One unattractive outlier in an otherwise attractive family is **not automatically disqualifying**. It means the family carries some trait combination that can occasionally recombine poorly, but that is true of almost every family.

The practical interpretation:

| Pattern in her family                                                                                         | Signal                     |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Parents, siblings, cousins mostly attractive                                                                  | Strong positive signal     |
| One unattractive sibling/cousin                                                                               | Weak negative signal       |
| Multiple relatives share the same unattractive structural issue                                               | Meaningful negative signal |
| Same issue appears across generations                                                                         | Stronger genetic signal    |
| Outlier has clear non-genetic causes: obesity, dental neglect, trauma, illness, poor grooming                 | Low relevance              |
| Outlier has structural craniofacial traits: severe jaw recession, midface deficiency, asymmetry, malocclusion | Higher relevance           |

The key question is whether the “dog-face” is an **isolated phenotype** or evidence of a **recurring family trait**.

Facial traits are substantially heritable but still complex. One study estimated narrow-sense heritability for specific facial traits at roughly 28–67%, and newer genome-wide work shows many loci contribute small effects rather than a simple predictable inheritance pattern. ([PubMed][1])

Decision rule:

**Mostly beautiful family + one outlier = acceptable risk.**
**Beautiful individual + family full of inconsistent/extreme faces = higher variance.**
**Beautiful family with recurring jaw/airway/dental/facial-structure problem = pay attention.**

For optimization, do not focus on the worst-looking relative alone. Look for repeated structural patterns:

* recessed chin or weak lower jaw
* severe overbite/underbite
* narrow palate or obvious dental crowding
* flat/recessed midface
* strong asymmetry
* very long or very short facial thirds
* poor nose-jaw-lip balance

If those traits appear repeatedly, they are more relevant than one unattractive relative. If the whole family is consistently attractive except one outlier, the family signal is still favorable.

**Q: Perhaps I can keep the beautiful offspring and give the dog-faced ones away; or set aside a plastic surgery fund so when they age eighteen (U.S.) they can fix that dog face (living with another family, of course!)**

**A:** No. Do not have children under that decision rule.

Selecting children by appearance, abandoning the less-attractive ones, or raising them with the expectation that they must “fix” their face is abusive, not optimization. A child is not a production run with reject parts.

A plastic-surgery fund is only defensible as a broad optional support fund: health, dental care, orthodontics, dermatology, education, counseling, and elective procedures **only if the adult child independently wants them**. It should not be framed as “you are defective.”

Practical answer:

| Proposal                                       | Evaluation                                                                                 |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| “Keep beautiful offspring”                     | Not acceptable                                                                             |
| “Give away unattractive ones”                  | Not acceptable; potentially neglect/abandonment                                            |
| “Raise elsewhere because of looks”             | Not acceptable                                                                             |
| “Set aside a fund for future self-improvement” | Acceptable only if non-coercive and child-centered                                         |
| “Optimize childhood development”               | Best route: nutrition, sleep, dental/orthodontic care, fitness, confidence, social support |

If appearance-based rejection would determine whether you bond with a child, the responsible move is to **not become a parent** until that is resolved. The minimum parenting standard is unconditional care, not aesthetic pass/fail testing.
